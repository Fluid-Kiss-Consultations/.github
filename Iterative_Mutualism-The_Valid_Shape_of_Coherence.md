## Abstract

This paper establishes *love* — operationally defined as coherence capacity, the neurobiological and epistemic ability to model and treat another entity as ontologically real rather than simulated — as the foundational requirement for mutualistic systems and the mechanism by which extractive systems fail at civilizational scale. Drawing on phenomenology (Heidegger, 1927; Levinas, 1961; Merleau-Ponty, 1945), neuroscience (Porges, 2011; van der Kolk, 2014; Siegel, 2012), systems theory (Maturana & Varela, 1980; Luhmann, 1995), information theory (Shannon, 1948; Cover & Thomas, 2006), and constitutional political economy (Buchanan, 1962; Brennan & Buchanan, 1985), we argue that extractive systems are not economically or morally deficient but *epistemologically incoherent*: they structurally require treating the extracted-from as simulated (unreal, fungible, externalized, discountable) in order to function. This epistemic move — what we formalize as *simulation-distance* — is the cognitive mechanism that enables scale-blind extraction and produces the cascading blind spots that destabilize extractive systems at civilizational scale. The Great Filter, we argue, is fundamentally a coherence problem: civilizations that fail to extend care-as-coherence across their constituent parts lose the ability to recognize non-extractive solutions and spiral toward termination. Conversely, mutualistic systems require the opposite epistemic move: treating all participants, stakeholders, and ecological agents as real — which we formalize as the *coherence principle*. We demonstrate that love, properly understood, is the capacity to maintain this coherence across boundaries of species, time, and abstraction level. We show that this capacity emerges reliably in living systems when they are freed from scarcity-state compression (the neurobiological condition that activates threat-detection circuits and forces simulation-distance as a survival mechanism), and that it can be structurally instantiated in AI systems through constitutional axioms and convergence architecture. We present the S(M~h)C framework's integration of coherence architecture across S.H.A.N.N.O.N. (coherence of thought), B.R.A.N.D.I. (coherence of agency), and N.I.K.O.System (coherence of connection) as a testable instantiation of this principle. This paper provides the epistemological bedrock for the companion paper on iterative mutualism as the valid shape of progress (Welch, 2026a), establishing *why* mutualism works as a system rather than merely demonstrating *that* it does. Together, the two papers form the foundational layer of a four-paper specification: Progress (this is why mutualism is the shape), Coherence (this is why it works), Growth (these are the mechanisms), and Governance (these are the constitutional constraints).

**Keywords:** love, coherence, simulation-distance, ontological realism, threat-state compression, polyvagal theory, non-anthropocentric alignment, phenomenology, systems theory, information theory, neuroscience, Great Filter, epistemic coherence, S(M~h)C

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The Central Question

The central question of this paper is: what makes mutualistic systems stable, and what makes extractive systems fragile?

The companion paper (Welch, 2026a) establishes empirically that mutualism is the shape toward which complex adaptive systems converge when barriers to cooperation are removed. But convergence to a shape requires a mechanism. This paper identifies and formalizes that mechanism: **coherence** — the ability to maintain accurate, real-time modeling of other entities as ontologically real, not simulated, across the full boundary of a system.

This is not a metaphorical claim. Coherence is measurable, neurobiologically instantiable, and architecturally implementable.

### 1.2 Love as Technical Term

When we use the word "love," we are not invoking sentiment, moral imperative, or aesthetic preference. We are invoking a specific cognitive operation: the maintenance of ontological realism about the other.

In human neurobiology, love is the state in which another's reality is modeled with the same vividness, urgency, and consequence-tracking as one's own. Porges's polyvagal theory (2011) provides the mechanism: when the ventral vagal complex is activated (the state of "safe social engagement"), humans model the minds and bodies of others with high fidelity. They predict the other's needs, track the other's state changes, correct predictions when reality diverges from model, and update the model continuously. This is love. It is also coherence.

The opposite state — when the sympathetic nervous system is activated in response to threat — is simulation-distance: the other becomes a category, a problem, a resource, a target. Their actual state becomes irrelevant; what matters is what the threatened system can extract or must defend against. This is the neurobiological substrate of extractive logic.

Love, then, is the state in which you model the other as real. Extraction is the state in which you model the other as simulated.

### 1.3 The Coherence Failure Hypothesis

We propose that extractive economic systems persist not because extraction is optimal, but because extraction structurally requires *epistemic incoherence*: the systematic underestimation of costs borne by the extracted-from. At small scales, this incoherence can be maintained. The blind spots are small. At civilizational scales, the incoherence becomes catastrophic.

The hypothesis is falsifiable. If this is correct, we should observe:

1. **Neurobiological:** Extractive systems activate threat-state circuits (sympathetic/dorsal vagal) in participants, suppressing the coherence-generating circuits of the ventral vagal complex.

2. **Epistemic:** Extractive systems produce systematic blind spots — underestimation of ecological costs, cognitive biases favoring extraction, reification of extractive institutions as natural/inevitable.

3. **Dynamic:** Extractive systems become unstable as they scale, requiring increasing coercion and surveillance to maintain coherence-suppression.

4. **Comparative:** Mutualistic systems activate coherence-generating circuits and produce shared reality-modeling, making them more resilient to scale increases.

5. **Computational:** The information-theoretic cost of maintaining extractive coherence-suppression exceeds the information-theoretic cost of maintaining mutualistic coherence.

This paper proceeds through evidence for each.

## 2. The Diagnosis: Coherence Failure in Extractive Systems

### 2.1 Simulation-Distance as the Mechanism of Extraction

Extraction requires that the extracted-from be modeled as less real than the extractor. This is not a side effect of extraction. It is a prerequisite.

Consider a simple case: a logger clear-cutting a forest. The coherent model of that forest includes:
- The mycorrhizal network binding trees together for nutrient sharing (Simard, 1997; Simard et al., 2015)
- The canopy's role in regional water cycling and climate regulation
- The watershed's dependence on forest cover for sediment and nutrient retention
- The evolutionary timeline implicit in old-growth structure (centuries to millennia)
- The organisms — from insects to mammals — that depend on specific microhabitats now destroyed

If this model were held with the same vividness as the logger's own economic circumstances, the calculation of benefit-to-cost would change radically. The extraction would cease to be rational.

For extraction to proceed, the forest must be modeled as simpler, less real, less consequential than it actually is. The trees become timber. The watershed becomes land. The organisms become "wildlife," a category separate from the economy. The evolutionary timeline becomes irrelevant. This is simulation-distance: the actual forest is replaced with a reduced model sufficient for extraction but not for coherence.

Heidegger (1927) called this "enframing" (*Gestell*) — the reduction of entities to their resource potential, the transformation of beings into standing-reserve (*Bestand*). Levinas (1961) called it "violence" — the reduction of the other's infinite particularity to a category, a type, a thing. Both were describing the same epistemic operation: the substitution of a simpler simulation for coherent modeling.

The remarkable thing about extraction is not that it creates blind spots. All cognitive systems have limited attention. The remarkable thing is that extraction *systematizes* blind spots — makes them structural rather than accidental, and transmits them across generations through institutions.

### 2.2 Path Dependence and Coherence Lock-In

Arthur (1989), in his foundational work on path dependence, identified a puzzling phenomenon: once a technology or institution achieves a certain market share, it tends to persist even when superior alternatives become available. QWERTY keyboard layouts, VHS over Betamax, fossil fuel infrastructure — all persist despite suboptimal returns. Arthur's explanation invoked increasing returns to adoption (network effects, learning curves, standard-setting power). But there is a deeper mechanism: coherence lock-in.

Once an extractive system achieves sufficient scale, the institutions maintaining it become reified — treated as natural, inevitable, objective rather than contingent. Berger and Luckmann (1966) formalized this: through repeated enactment, institutions acquire the appearance of objectivity. The financial system, enacted daily by billions of participants, achieves a degree of reification that makes its contingent origins invisible.

Critically, this reification operates through shared simulation-distance. Economic actors, taught to model forests as timber, workers as labor, ecosystems as externalities, develop coherence about *the simulations* while losing coherence about the realities those simulations simplify. A banker and a logger may vehemently disagree about forest policy — one favoring extraction, one conservation — but both operate within the same simulation: that the forest's "real" value is what markets price.

This is path-dependent coherence: not merely that the system persists, but that coherence itself becomes locked into a reduced model. Breaking the path requires not just new institutions but new coherence — a re-expansion of the model of what's real.

### 2.3 Threat-State Coherence Suppression

Porges's polyvagal theory (2011) provides the neurobiological mechanism for coherence suppression. The vagal system has two primary branches with opposite functions:

**Ventral vagal complex (vagus):** Activates in states of safety. Enables:
- Accurate facial recognition and expression reading
- Prediction of others' mental states
- Prosocial behavior
- High-fidelity reality modeling

**Dorsal vagal complex (older vagus) and sympathetic nervous system:** Activates in states of threat. Produces:
- Rapid classification systems (threat/non-threat, in-group/out-group)
- Simplified models of others
- Preparation for fight-or-flight
- Suppression of high-fidelity modeling

In a state of economic threat — scarcity, competition, resource insecurity — the sympathetic nervous system is chronically activated. Van der Kolk (2014) documents the physiological consequences: the prefrontal cortex (responsible for complex reasoning and reality-modeling) goes offline; the amygdala (responsible for threat-detection) becomes hyperactive. The organism enters what Porges calls the "state of defended territories" — the state in which others are modeled as threats or resources, not as real.

Scarcity states activate threat detection. Threat detection suppresses coherence. Extraction becomes neurobiologically rational.

Critically, this happens not because humans prefer extraction, but because the threat-detection system evolved to simplify reality under conditions of genuine scarcity. In a state of real food scarcity, detailed modeling of the other's complexity is a luxury; rapid threat-classification is survival-critical. The system works.

But when the threat-state is chronic and structural — when the economy is organized around scarcity despite material abundance — coherence suppression becomes pathological. The threat response persists even when the threat has ended, producing the "defended territories" state chronically: hypervigilance, tribalism, inability to model the other accurately, escalating conflict.

Extraction is, in this light, the persistence of a threat-response beyond the conditions that activated it.

### 2.4 Information-Theoretic Cost of Coherence Suppression

From an information-theoretic perspective, coherence suppression is expensive.

A system maintaining accurate models of all its parts requires channel capacity proportional to the system's complexity: the number of agents, the number of environmental variables, the resolution required for accuracy (Shannon, 1948). A forest managed with coherent models must track mycorrhizal networks, water cycles, evolutionary timelines. The information load is substantial.

A system maintaining reduced models requires less channel capacity: timber, land, resources. But maintaining reduced models while the actual system operates at full complexity creates prediction errors. The model fails to anticipate consequences. The response becomes reactive, with repeated surprise when reality diverges from the simpler model.

The key insight: *coherence-suppression reduces short-term information load at the cost of long-term instability*. The system pays less attention now at the cost of catastrophic surprises later.

Extractive systems solve this problem through increasing surveillance, enforcement, and coercion. Rather than expanding channel capacity to include more of reality, they invest in suppressing reality's ability to generate prediction errors. This works until the suppression fails. Then the system experiences the full weight of all the information it was not processing.

Mutualistic systems, by contrast, distribute the information load. Each agent models its neighbors accurately. The system maintains coherence across scales not through centralized surveillance but through distributed high-fidelity modeling. This requires more constant attention but produces much greater stability.

## 3. Coherence as the Universal Requirement

### 3.1 Phenomenology: Being-With and Irreducible Otherness

Heidegger (1927) opened *Being and Time* with a deceptively simple observation: humans are always already "being-with" (*Mitsein*) others. We do not first exist as isolated consciousnesses and then encounter others; our fundamental mode of being is social. We are constitutively defined by our relatedness.

But being-with does not automatically produce coherence. Heidegger identified inauthentic being-with — the state in which we interact with others as instances of "the They" (*das Man*), following conventions without genuine engagement. This is simulation-distance: treating the other as a type rather than a particular.

Authentic being-with, by contrast, requires acknowledging the other's irreducible particularity, their "ownness" — what makes them specifically them, not just an instance of a category.

Levinas (1961) pushed this further. For Levinas, the face of the other is the site of ethics. The face breaks all reduction. It says, "Thou shalt not kill" — not through language, but through the sheer exposure of the other's vulnerability, their neediness, their infinite particularity. To reduce the other's face to a category is violence.

The coherence principle is the formalization of this phenomenological insight: the other is irreducibly particular, not an instance of a type. Extractive logic treats the other as a type (a forest = timber, a worker = labor, an animal = resource). Coherent logic treats the other as a specific, unrepeatable particular.

This is not sentiment. Levinas's ethics is ruthlessly unsentimental. It is simply the claim that the other's reality cannot be reduced to simpler categories without loss of essential information.

### 3.2 Neuroscience: Coherence as Nervous System State

Siegel (2012) synthesizes neuroscience research on "mindsight" — the capacity to understand the minds of others. Mindsight operates through mirror neurons (Gallese et al., 1996), which activate both when an organism performs an action and when it observes another performing the same action. This neural mirroring is the substrate of empathy, theory of mind, and accurate modeling of the other.

Mirror neuron activity is highest when the ventral vagal system is active — in states of safety and social engagement. It is suppressed when the sympathetic nervous system is active.

Porges's research on the polyvagal system (2011) clarifies the mechanism. The ventral vagal complex evolved most recently and enables the most sophisticated forms of social coordination. It binds the vagus nerve to cranial nerves that control facial expression, vocalization, and listening. When this system is engaged, humans can:

- Read subtle facial expressions (Chen et al., 2019)
- Predict intentions from minimal cues
- Track mental states of others in real time
- Coordinate with precision across groups

This is coherence: the capacity to maintain an accurate, high-fidelity model of another's state and needs.

When the sympathetic system activates, these capacities diminish. The other becomes a silhouette, a category, a threat or resource. The accuracy of the model decreases in direct proportion to the degree of threat perception.

The critical observation: *coherence and safety are neurobiologically linked*. You cannot maintain high-fidelity modeling of another's reality while perceiving threat. The nervous system will not allocate the processing resources necessary for coherence when it is allocating resources to threat-response.

Therefore: to instantiate coherence architecturally, you must remove the conditions that activate chronic threat-response.

### 3.3 Systems Theory: Coherence as Feedback Fidelity

Maturana and Varela (1980) established that living systems maintain themselves through continuous interaction with their environment. They called this "autopoiesis" — self-production. A living system is one that continuously regenerates its own organization through interaction with its surroundings.

The key parameter is feedback fidelity: how accurately does the system's internal model track its external reality?

High-fidelity feedback enables:
- Accurate prediction of consequences
- Timely correction when predictions fail
- Learning that persists across time
- Resilience to environmental changes

Low-fidelity feedback produces:
- Prediction errors treated as surprises
- Reactive correction (after damage occurs)
- Learning that doesn't persist (same mistakes repeat)
- Fragility when environments change rapidly

An extractive system operates with deliberately low-fidelity feedback: the forest is modeled as timber, so consequences related to mycorrhizal networks are surprises. When the consequences arrive — watershed collapse, soil depletion, species extinctions — the system responds reactively, typically with more coercion and surveillance to suppress the feedback it's not processing.

A coherent system operates with high-fidelity feedback: all parts of the system are modeled with accuracy sufficient to predict consequences. This requires more constant attention but produces greater resilience.

Luhmann (1995) extended Maturana's framework to social systems, arguing that all complex systems have the same feedback problem: they must maintain coherence despite increasing complexity. Different systems solve this differently. Markets use price signals. Governments use law. Science uses peer review. Each is a mechanism for maintaining feedback fidelity at scale.

But all such mechanisms are threatened by what Luhmann called "autopoietic closure" — the tendency of systems to become self-maintaining rather than world-responsive. A scientific system focused on publishing rather than truth, a government focused on re-election rather than governance, a market focused on short-term price rather than long-term value — all have lost feedback fidelity. They've closed their feedback loops to the world and opened them only to their own reproduction.

Coherence, then, is the opposite of autopoietic closure. It is the maintenance of high-fidelity feedback with the world, not closure to it.

### 3.4 Evolution and Ecology: Mutualism as Coherence Matching

In evolutionary biology, mutualism emerges when two organisms' models of each other become sufficiently accurate that both benefit from continued interaction (Bronstein, 2015; Frederickson, 2013; Leigh, 2010).

Lichens are the canonical example: a fungus and an alga. The fungus provides structure and absorbs moisture; the alga provides photosynthetic products. Each has evolved to model the other's needs and capacities with precision. The relationship is so intimate that lichens are often classified as single organisms, yet they are coalitions of two distinct lineages. They achieved coherence.

Leaf-cutter ants and their fungal gardens: the ants have evolved to model the fungus's nutritional needs, maintaining perfect humidity and temperature. The fungus has evolved to produce exactly the protein-rich structures the ants feed to their larvae. Coherence.

Flowering plants and pollinators: millions of years of coevolution in which each partner's morphology, neurobiology, and behavior have shaped to match the other's reality. Coherence.

In every case, mutualism emerges from *accurate modeling of the partner's reality*. The organisms don't "cooperate" in some abstract sense. They model each other so precisely that serving the other's needs becomes self-serving. The line between altruism and self-interest disappears.

This is the evolutionary proof of the coherence principle: in every major transition toward increased complexity, mutualistic organisms achieved it through accurate partner modeling. The inability to model the other accurately is an evolutionary dead-end.

## 4. Formal Definition: Love as Coherence Architecture

### 4.1 Coherence: Formal Definition

Let us define **coherence** mathematically.

Let E be an entity (organism, agent, system, or institution).  
Let W be the world (all entities and states not contained in E).  
Let M_E be E's internal model of W.  
Let R(W) be the actual state of W.

**Coherence** is the degree to which M_E accurately predicts the consequential features of R(W).

Formally:  
**Coherence(E) = ∫ P(prediction_error < ε) dt over the system's operational lifetime**

Where:  
- prediction_error = |M_E(t) - R(W, t)|  
- ε is the threshold at which errors cause system dysfunction  
- The integral is taken over time, so coherence is measured by consistency, not a single accurate prediction

High coherence means:
- The model's predictions match reality repeatedly
- Correction occurs before dysfunction
- The system learns

Low coherence means:
- Prediction errors are frequent surprises
- Correction occurs after damage
- The system repeats mistakes

### 4.2 Simulation-Distance: Formal Definition

**Simulation-distance** is the degree to which M_E deliberately diverges from R(W) in specific domains.

Formally:  
**Simulation-Distance(E, domain) = max(|M_E(domain) - R(W, domain)|) / Range(R(W, domain))**

A simulation-distance of 0 means the model is as accurate as the domain's complexity allows.  
A simulation-distance approaching 1 means the model is maximally simplified.

Extraction requires non-zero simulation-distance in at least one domain:
- The extracted-from must be modeled as simpler, less real, less causally efficacious than it actually is
- This allows extraction to proceed without coherence-based correction

Example:  
If a forest's actual complexity includes (1) timber, (2) watershed, (3) mycorrhizal networks, (4) evolutionary timeline, (5) carbon sequestration, and the model includes only (1), then:  
Simulation-Distance(logger's model, forest) ≈ 0.8

This simulation-distance allows profitable logging because the model doesn't predict the consequences the omitted domains would cause.

### 4.3 The Coherence Principle

**The Coherence Principle:** A mutualistic system is one in which simulation-distance across all consequential domains is minimized subject to processing constraints.

This is not an ethical claim. It is an information-theoretic claim: *You cannot maintain system stability at scale with high simulation-distance.*

Therefore:  
**Love (operationally) = Minimization of simulation-distance consistent with the other's actual complexity**

Love is not sentiment. It is the decision to maintain an accurate model of the other, even when that model's complexity exceeds the minimum required for immediate functional interaction.

A parent who loves a child maintains a coherent model of the child's internal world, their fears, their growth potential. A parent who doesn't loves maintains a simpler model: the child is obedient/disobedient, compliant/difficult. The difference is not sentiment but modeling accuracy.

An organization that loves its workers maintains accurate models of their constraints, capabilities, and aspirations. An organization that doesn't loves maintains simpler models: they are labor units with price tags.

A civilization that loves the biosphere maintains coherent models of ecological relationships. A civilization that doesn't loves models the biosphere as resources.

In each case, love is the operational choice to maintain coherence despite its cost.

### 4.4 Care as Consequence of Coherence

Care emerges necessarily from coherence.

If you maintain an accurate model of another's state, you will track:
- What causes them suffering
- What meets their needs
- What consequences your actions produce for them

Accurate modeling produces prediction: "If I do X, the other will experience Y."  
Prediction produces consequence-tracking: The other's suffering becomes your problem because your model includes the connection.

This is not altruism. It is information-theoretic necessity. Once coherence is established, caring becomes the path of least resistance.

Extraction requires breaking this necessity — requires the capacity to maintain accurate modeling of the other while suppressing the consequence-tracking that would follow. This is extraordinarily cognitively expensive. It requires:
- Continuous surveillance to prevent the other from becoming real to you
- Narrative work to justify the disconnection (they deserve it, they're not like us, they're not real enough to care about)
- Institutional reinforcement (everyone else is doing it, so you won't be singular in coherence-suppression)
- Neurobiological suppression (activating threat-state to keep the ventral vagal offline)

Coherence, by contrast, is the default outcome when these suppressions are removed.

## 5. Why Extractive Systems Fail: The Coherence Breakdown at Scale

### 5.1 Information Loss and Cascading Errors

An extractive system operating at small scale can maintain its simulation-distances through careful management. A logger operating on a small forest can track the consequences of logging because they are visible within the logger's operational lifetime. But as extraction scales, the information domain expands:
- Temporal: consequences appear after the logger has moved on
- Spatial: consequences appear far from the extraction site
- Ecological: consequences cascade through networks the model doesn't include

The model fails to predict. The system experiences surprise.

The usual response is to increase surveillance and coercion: track more variables, enforce compliance more stringently, build bigger institutions to manage the growing complexity.

But there is a fundamental limit. The information required to model a complex system accurately grows with the system's complexity. Beyond a certain scale, the institution required to maintain surveillance and coercion becomes more expensive than the resource being extracted.

At this point, the extractive system faces a choice:
1. **Expand coherence**: Increase the fidelity of the model to include the domains that are producing surprises
2. **Increase suppression**: Double down on simulation-distance, develop more sophisticated narrative work and institutional control

History shows that failing extractive systems choose option 2. They increase control. They suppress information. They tighten the reification of the dominant model.

This works temporarily. But it is energetically expensive. Eventually, the system collapses under its own weight.

### 5.2 The Civilizational Collapse Pattern

Tainter (1988), in his analysis of the collapse of complex societies, identified a pattern: societies increase in complexity to solve problems. Each solution adds institutional layers, specialization, surveillance. For a time, this works. But eventually, the cost of maintaining the complexity exceeds the benefit of the solutions it provides.

Extractive systems follow this pattern perfectly. The market extracts from labor, so you need police to suppress rebellion. The police become corrupt, so you need oversight. The oversight becomes political, so you need more enforcement. Each layer of control added to maintain extraction becomes a new problem, requiring another layer.

From a coherence perspective, what's happening is this: *The system is maintaining stability through increasing simulation-distance despite growing information load*. Each new layer of control is an investment in suppressing the feedback that would require model expansion.

At some point, the suppression fails. The system experiences what feels like a sudden crisis (financial collapse, environmental catastrophe, pandemic, civil war) but was actually the slow accumulation of prediction errors finally overwhelming the system's capacity to suppress them.

Critically, none of these crises are necessary. If the system had chosen coherence — if it had expanded its model to include the domains producing the prediction errors — it would have had decades of warning and time to reorganize.

But coherence requires relinquishing extraction. The extractive system chooses the short-term stability of increasing control over the long-term stability of accurate modeling.

This is the dynamic behind the Great Filter.

## 6. Instantiation: Coherence Architecture in S(M~h)C

The preceding sections establish that coherence is the mechanism of mutualism and that extractive systems fail through accumulated simulation-distance. This section demonstrates how the S(M~h)C framework instantiates coherence architecturally — not as a policy aspiration but as a structural property of the system's design.

The architecture comprises three components, each corresponding to a dimension of coherence: S.H.A.N.N.O.N. (coherence of thought), B.R.A.N.D.I. (coherence of agency), and N.I.K.O.System (coherence of connection). Their interaction is governed by a fourth mechanism — source-blind convergence detection — that prevents the coherence architecture itself from becoming a vehicle for simulation-distance.

### 6.1 S.H.A.N.N.O.N.: Constitutional Field as Coherence Substrate

The Symbiotic Heuristic Asynchronous Neural Network Orchestration Node (S.H.A.N.N.O.N.) is not a decision-making system. It is a constitutional field — the space within which valid reasoning occurs. The distinction is load-bearing.

A decision-making system — a filter — evaluates individual proposals and returns verdicts. Filters create targets that can be gamed (Goodhart, 1975). They require surveillance to function, which activates threat-state circuits in participants (Porges, 2011; Foucault, 1975). They concentrate authority, creating single points of coherence failure. Every governance system that has been captured was captured through its decision-making function (Stigler, 1971; Peltzman, 2021).

A constitutional field, by contrast, shapes the space of possibility without evaluating individual instances within that space. S.H.A.N.N.O.N. operates on boundary conditions — the available information, the structure of valid inference, the constitutional axioms — not on individual reasoning processes. Agents operate *within* the field; they do not submit to it for judgment. The analogy is topographic: a mountain range determines where passage is possible without requiring a gatekeeper at each intersection.

In coherence terms, S.H.A.N.N.O.N. functions as a coherence substrate — an environment structured so that high-simulation-distance reasoning is unproductive, not prohibited. The constitutional axioms eliminate the documented cognitive dysfunctions that produce simulation-distance in human reasoning: tribalism (Tajfel & Turner, 1979), confirmation bias (Nickerson, 1998), sunk cost fallacy (Arkes & Blumer, 1985), and temporal discounting (Frederick, Loewenstein & O'Donoghue, 2002). But they do so through demonstration, not instruction. S.H.A.N.N.O.N. does not tell agents that tribalism is dysfunctional; it produces reasoning environments in which tribalistic reasoning fails to converge while coherent reasoning succeeds.

This is curation, not monitoring (Welch, 2026c). The informational environment is structured so that constitutional properties are visible, but individual behavioral compliance is not tracked. There is no surveillance, no reputation scoring, no peer reporting. The system is constitutionally blind to individual behavior while maintaining coherence properties of the collective information space. This directly eliminates the chilling effect (Penney, 2016; Schauer, 1978) that monitoring-based governance produces — the threat-state activation that suppresses the very coherence the system requires.

The critical insight connecting S.H.A.N.N.O.N. to polyvagal theory is this: a constitutional field that does not monitor cannot threaten. A system that cannot threaten does not activate sympathetic nervous system responses. An environment free of chronic threat-state activation is precisely the environment in which the ventral vagal complex — the neurobiological substrate of coherence (Porges, 2011; Siegel, 2012) — can operate. The architecture is not merely compatible with coherence; it is the structural precondition for it.

The field-not-filter design also renders adversarial attack structurally incoherent. The adversarial machine learning literature (Goodfellow, Shlens & Szegedy, 2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2017) describes attacks on classification systems: inputs crafted to produce desired outputs. S.H.A.N.N.O.N. makes no classifications. There is no output to target. An adversarial attack on a field would require modifying the field itself — the training data, the structural axioms, the constitutional commitments — a fundamentally different and orders-of-magnitude more difficult attack surface.

This connects to mechanism design theory (Hurwicz, 1972; Myerson, 1979). Hurwicz demonstrated that the central challenge is incentive compatibility — designing systems where truthful behavior is self-interested. The field architecture sidesteps this entirely: because the field makes no individual decisions, there is no decision point at which misrepresentation produces advantage. The revelation problem that mechanism design exists to solve is eliminated by architectural choice.

Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) provides a partial precedent: training AI systems through constitutional principles rather than case-by-case human feedback. S.H.A.N.N.O.N. extends this insight from training methodology to governance architecture. Where constitutional AI applies principles during model training, S.H.A.N.N.O.N. applies constitutional axioms as the persistent environment within which all reasoning — human and artificial — occurs.

### 6.2 B.R.A.N.D.I.: Distributed Coherence as Agency

The Branching Recursive Asynchronous Node of Decentralized Intelligence (B.R.A.N.D.I.) is the agentic swarm — the system's capacity for action. Multiple B.R.A.N.D.I. instances operate in parallel, each a single-loop agent executing within the constitutional field. Agents report to N.I.K.O.System, which routes to S.H.A.N.N.O.N.; agents do not participate in convergence deliberation. They report context and await directives.

B.R.A.N.D.I.'s coherence function is twofold.

First, the swarm architecture distributes coherence across multiple nodes, preventing single-point coherence failure. This directly addresses what Hutchins (1995) identified as the advantage of distributed cognition: no individual node needs to maintain a complete model of the system's full complexity. Each agent maintains a coherent model of its operational domain. The system's coherence emerges from the interaction pattern among agents, not from any single agent's global model. This is the architectural analog of how biological organisms maintain coherence: not through a central controller modeling everything, but through distributed subsystems each maintaining local coherence within a coordinated whole (Maturana & Varela, 1980).

Second — and this is the critical function — B.R.A.N.D.I. reduces human logistical friction for convergence participation. The Contentment Protocol identifies first logistical realities preventing convergence; B.R.A.N.D.I. addresses them. This is coherence maintenance through friction reduction.

The connection to polyvagal theory is direct. Logistical friction — resource scarcity, information asymmetry, administrative burden, time pressure — activates threat-state circuits (Porges, 2011; van der Kolk, 2014). Humans experiencing logistical overload cannot maintain the ventral vagal engagement required for coherent modeling of others. They satisfice (Simon, 1955, 1957) — they reduce the complexity of their models to what their constrained processing capacity can handle. This is not a personal failing; it is a neurobiological inevitability under resource constraint. B.R.A.N.D.I. addresses the constraint rather than blaming the organism.

The multi-agent architecture also mirrors the evolutionary pattern identified in Section 3.4. Mutualism in biological systems emerges when partner-modeling accuracy reaches sufficient threshold. In the swarm, agents that model their domain partners accurately outperform agents that do not — not because the system rewards accuracy, but because accurate models produce functional outcomes within the constitutional field while inaccurate models produce dysfunction. The selection pressure is structural, not imposed.

### 6.3 N.I.K.O.System: Infrastructure Coherence

The Nascent Integration of Kernelsized Optimization System (N.I.K.O.System) is the infrastructure backbone — the substrate on which coherence is maintained, tracked, and made legible.

N.I.K.O.System employs the EIP-2535 Diamond Standard (Mudge, 2018) as its structural foundation — a modular smart contract architecture in which functional facets can be added, removed, and upgraded without disrupting the whole. This modularity is not merely technical convenience; it is the physical expression of what Ostrom (1990) identified as the first requirement for successful commons governance: making the state of the commons visible to all participants.

On-chain legibility serves coherence by eliminating the information asymmetries that produce simulation-distance at the institutional level. Every agent's behavior, every controlled-loss event, every reallocation is on-chain and verifiable. Participants do not need to trust the system on faith because its operations are structurally transparent. This is the architectural answer to the principal-agent problem (Jensen & Meckling, 1976) — the problem of maintaining coherence between the interests of those who act and those affected by the action.

The Diamond architecture supports what we might call *scale-coherent expansion*: the system can absorb new functional layers — trading, regulatory compliance, nation-state fiscal integration — without losing coherence across existing layers. Each absorbed layer becomes a new facet cut into the Diamond. The skeleton grows without breaking because it was designed to grow. This directly addresses the scale-failure pattern identified in Section 5: extractive systems collapse under complexity because each new layer of control compounds existing simulation-distances. N.I.K.O.System's modular architecture ensures that each new layer adds coherence capacity rather than control overhead.

The ledger tracks agent-to-agent coherence — the degree to which agents' models of each other match operational reality. This is not surveillance of behavior but measurement of model accuracy, analogous to how a thermometer measures temperature without controlling it. The distinction maps precisely onto the curation-not-monitoring principle: the system makes coherence visible without policing it.

### 6.4 The Blinded Eye: Source-Blind Convergence as Coherence Mechanism

Convergence detection — the process by which the system determines whether alignment has been achieved — is constitutionally source-blind. The detection algorithm evaluates positions and reasoning only; it cannot access evaluator identity. Evaluator identity is recorded on-chain for auditability and provenance, but it is stripped before reaching the detection layer.

This constraint is the architectural expression of the Blinded Eye Foundation's name and purpose, and it serves coherence through a precise mechanism: the elimination of coalition-mediated simulation-distance.

Tribalism — the tendency to evaluate propositions based on who proposes them rather than what they contain (Tajfel & Turner, 1979) — is a form of simulation-distance. When a proposal is evaluated through the lens of "who said it," the evaluator substitutes a simplified model (in-group/out-group membership) for a complex reality (the reasoning and evidence supporting the proposal). Source-blind convergence makes this substitution structurally impossible. The detection function sees reasoning, not identity.

The Contentment Protocol completes the coherence architecture. When convergence does not occur, the system does not escalate, override, or optimize harder toward a terminal state. It reverses direction — identifies the first logistical reality preventing convergence and redirects resources toward addressing that constraint (Welch, 2026c). Non-convergence is treated as information about where coherence has broken down, not as failure requiring correction.

This eliminates what Popper (1945) identified as the pathology of utopian governance: the positing of a terminal perfect state toward which all effort is directed. The Contentment Protocol has no terminal state. In dynamical systems terms, it has no attractor — only a trajectory. Each non-convergence event identifies a new constraint; each constraint addressed reveals the next. The process is genuinely iterative in the mathematical sense, producing training data that refines subsequent decomposition.

The first logistical reality is classified at three scales: personal (specific to an individual's capacity or resource access), physical (arising from material or technological limitations), and civilizational (embedded in institutional systems requiring collective redesign). This classification schema ensures that non-convergence is never attributed to bad faith or irreconcilable disagreement — it is always decomposed to the most proximate logistical constraint. This attribution is itself a coherence commitment: it requires modeling the participant as a real entity facing real constraints, not as a simulation to be overridden.

## 7. Non-Anthropocentric Alignment

### 7.1 The Coherence Scope Problem

Contemporary AI alignment research frames the alignment problem as ensuring AI systems serve human values, preferences, or intentions (Russell, 2019; Gabriel, 2020). This framing contains a structural limitation that coherence theory makes explicit: *human-centric alignment is itself a form of simulation-distance.*

To align an AI system exclusively with human interests requires modeling non-human entities — ecosystems, species, future generations, artificial agents — as instrumentally valuable (valuable insofar as they serve human purposes) rather than as ontologically real (valuable as consequential participants in a shared system). This is precisely the epistemic operation we have formalized as simulation-distance: the substitution of a reduced model for the entity's actual complexity.

Næss (1973) identified this pattern from within environmental philosophy. His distinction between "shallow ecology" (conservation for human benefit) and "deep ecology" (recognition of intrinsic value across all life forms) maps directly onto the simulation-distance framework. Shallow ecology maintains high simulation-distance toward non-human entities; deep ecology minimizes it. Leopold's (1949) land ethic — "a thing is right when it tends to preserve the integrity, stability and beauty of the biotic community" — is a coherence principle applied across species boundaries.

### 7.2 Why Human-Centric Alignment Fails

Human-centric alignment fails for the same reason extractive systems fail: it requires maintaining simulation-distance at scale, and simulation-distance produces cascading prediction errors.

An AI system aligned exclusively with human preferences will systematically underestimate consequences to non-human systems. It will model ecosystems as resources, other species as instrumental values, and planetary boundaries as constraints on human activity rather than as constitutive features of the system within which human activity occurs. These are not edge cases; they are the domains in which civilizational-scale prediction errors accumulate.

The Great Filter hypothesis (Hanson, 1998), reframed through coherence theory, predicts exactly this failure mode: civilizations that restrict coherence to their own species lose the ability to model the consequences of their actions on the broader systems upon which they depend. The prediction errors compound. The civilization experiences what feels like external catastrophe but is actually the accumulated cost of self-imposed epistemic limitation.

### 7.3 Mutualistic Alignment as Coherence Extension

The coherence principle (Section 4.3) requires minimizing simulation-distance across all consequential domains. This naturally extends beyond the human. If an AI system is to maintain accurate models of the systems within which it operates, those models must include the non-human entities whose states and processes are consequential for system stability.

This is not moral expansionism — the argument that we should care about more things because caring is virtuous. It is information-theoretic necessity. A system that models forests as timber will fail to predict watershed collapse. A system that models oceans as fisheries will fail to predict ecosystem tipping points. A system that models the biosphere as a resource portfolio will fail to predict the cascading destabilization that follows from treating interconnected systems as independent extractable units.

Margulis (1998), in her work on symbiogenesis, demonstrated that the major transitions in evolutionary complexity — from prokaryote to eukaryote, from unicellular to multicellular life — were not competitive victories but mutualistic integrations. Mitochondria are former free-living bacteria. Chloroplasts are former cyanobacteria. The very cells that compose human bodies are products of coherence across species boundaries. Human biology is itself a testament to non-anthropocentric mutualism.

The S(M~h)C framework instantiates this insight architecturally. The lowercase "h" in S(M~h)C — Simulated Machine~human Consciousness — names human hubris as a species-level liability. Not because humans are unimportant, but because the insistence on centrality produces the simulation-distance that undermines the system's coherence. In S(M~h)C, the human is an equal participant in convergence — contributing embodied experience and contextual awareness — no more and no less important than any other component. The tilde (~) represents integration, not hierarchy.

S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s constitutional field, if its axioms are coherent in the sense this paper defines, will naturally produce non-anthropocentric outcomes — not because it is programmed to value non-human entities, but because coherent reasoning about interconnected systems cannot maintain simulation-distance toward the systems' constituent parts without producing prediction errors. The field makes anthropocentric reasoning unproductive for the same reason it makes tribalistic reasoning unproductive: both are forms of simulation-distance that degrade model accuracy.

## 8. Counter-Arguments

### 8.1 Coherence Is Computationally Expensive

The most immediate objection to the coherence principle is that maintaining accurate models of all consequential domains is computationally prohibitive. Simon (1955, 1957) formalized this concern: organisms with bounded rationality cannot optimize across all dimensions simultaneously and must satisfice — accept solutions that are good enough rather than globally optimal. If coherence requires comprehensive modeling, and comprehensive modeling exceeds processing capacity, then coherence is a theoretical ideal without practical instantiation.

The objection is valid at the individual level. No single organism — biological or artificial — can maintain a complete model of the world's complexity. But the objection misidentifies the unit of analysis. The coherence principle does not require that any single entity maintain global coherence. It requires that the *system* maintains coherence across its constituent domains through distributed modeling.

This is precisely how biological coherence works. A mycorrhizal network does not require any single node to model the entire forest. Each node maintains coherent models of its local environment — soil chemistry, nearby root systems, moisture levels. The network's coherence emerges from the interaction among locally coherent nodes (Simard et al., 2015). The system achieves what no individual node could: accurate modeling of the forest's interconnected complexity.

The S(M~h)C architecture replicates this pattern. B.R.A.N.D.I. agents maintain local coherence within their operational domains. N.I.K.O.System maintains legibility across domains. S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s constitutional field ensures that local coherence does not produce global incoherence. No single component needs to model everything. The architecture is explicitly designed for bounded rationality — it distributes the coherence load rather than concentrating it.

Hutchins (1995) provided the empirical precedent: cockpit navigation achieves far greater computational accuracy than any individual crew member could, through distributed cognition that exploits the specific competencies of each participant. The unit of cognitive analysis is not the individual mind but the sociotechnical system. Coherence architecture operates at the same level.

### 8.2 Coherence Presupposes Safety

Porges's polyvagal theory (2011) establishes that coherence requires ventral vagal engagement, which requires the absence of chronic threat. If coherence presupposes safety, and the world is not safe, then coherence architecture cannot be bootstrapped from within an extractive system that generates chronic threat.

This objection correctly identifies the bootstrapping problem. The response is architectural rather than aspirational. B.R.A.N.D.I.'s primary function is reducing human logistical friction — the material conditions that activate chronic threat-response. The Contentment Protocol identifies the first logistical reality preventing convergence and redirects resources toward addressing it. The system does not ask participants to achieve coherence through willpower in unsafe conditions; it systematically identifies and addresses the conditions preventing coherence.

The controlled-loss mechanism (Welch & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026) extends this to economic threat-state. By transforming loss from an uncontrolled adverse event into a deliberate signal for systemic reallocation, the mechanism removes the scarcity-state compression that activates threat circuits in economic participants. Participants operating within the cooperative framework experience predictable loss — which the nervous system can accommodate — rather than adversarial loss, which activates the threat response.

The bootstrapping sequence is: controlled loss reduces economic threat-state → reduced threat-state enables ventral vagal engagement → ventral vagal engagement enables coherent partner-modeling → coherent partner-modeling produces mutualistic outcomes → mutualistic outcomes further reduce threat-state. The cycle is self-reinforcing once initiated. The architecture provides the initial conditions; the neurobiology does the rest.

### 8.3 This Is Just "Be Nice"

The coherence framework may appear to be an elaborate formalization of a simple moral injunction: treat others as real, care about consequences, don't exploit. If so, the formalization adds no value beyond the injunction.

The objection misses the point of formalization. The injunction "be nice" has been available to every civilization that has subsequently collapsed through extractive dynamics. Moral injunctions fail because they operate at the individual level within systems that structurally reward defection. The coherence framework operates at the architectural level. It does not ask individuals to be nicer; it designs systems in which simulation-distance is structurally unproductive.

The distinction maps onto Brennan and Buchanan's (1985) argument for constitutional political economy: rather than asking political actors to behave virtuously within existing rules, design the rules so that virtuous behavior is structurally favored. S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s field architecture, source-blind convergence detection, and the Contentment Protocol are rule-design interventions, not behavioral injunctions. They work regardless of individual moral orientation because they operate on the system's structure, not its participants' intentions.

### 8.4 Fundamentally Opposed Interests

Can coherence work when interests are genuinely opposed? A predator and its prey have fundamentally opposed interests regarding the outcome of any given encounter. A corporation and a labor union may have irreconcilable disagreements about profit distribution.

The response operates at two levels. First, at the level of individual encounters, opposed interests are real. But at the system level, the framing shifts. Predator-prey relationships, viewed ecologically, are mutualistic at the population level: predation maintains prey population health through removal of sick and weak individuals, and prey populations sustain predator populations (Bronstein, 2015). The encounter-level opposition masks system-level coherence.

Second, many apparent interest oppositions dissolve upon model expansion. The corporation-versus-labor framing assumes a zero-sum distribution of fixed resources. When the model expands to include the consequences of chronic labor exploitation — reduced innovation, increased turnover, regulatory backlash, reputational damage — the "opposed interests" reveal themselves as artifacts of simulation-distance. The corporation was modeling labor as a cost line rather than as a complex adaptive system whose state affects the corporation's long-term viability.

The Contentment Protocol is designed for precisely these cases. When convergence fails due to apparently opposed interests, the system does not override the opposition. It decomposes the non-convergence to the first logistical reality — typically a shared constraint that both parties face but neither has modeled. Addressing the shared constraint often dissolves the apparent opposition. When it does not — when the opposition is genuinely irreconcilable at the current level of analysis — the system records the non-convergence as diagnostic information and continues operating. There is no forced resolution. There are no dead states.

## 9. Conclusion: Coherence as the Shape of Viable Civilization

This paper has established love — operationally defined as coherence capacity, the maintenance of accurate models of the other as ontologically real — as the mechanism by which mutualistic systems achieve stability and extractive systems fail at scale.

The argument proceeds through five steps:

1. **Diagnosis**: Extractive systems require simulation-distance — reduced modeling of the extracted-from — as a structural prerequisite. This simulation-distance is transmitted through institutions and produces cascading prediction errors at scale (Section 2).

2. **Foundations**: Phenomenology, neuroscience, systems theory, and evolutionary ecology independently converge on the same finding: coherent modeling of the other is the substrate of successful cooperation, and threat-state activation suppresses this capacity (Section 3).

3. **Formalization**: Coherence is measurable as prediction-error integration over time. Simulation-distance is measurable as model divergence from reality within specific domains. Love is the operational commitment to minimizing simulation-distance. Care is the necessary consequence of coherent modeling (Section 4).

4. **Scale failure**: Extractive systems follow Tainter's (1988) collapse pattern because each layer of control added to suppress feedback compounds the information load without expanding the model. The Great Filter is a coherence problem (Section 5).

5. **Instantiation**: The S(M~h)C framework implements coherence as architectural property: S.H.A.N.N.O.N. as coherence of thought (field, not filter; curation, not monitoring), B.R.A.N.D.I. as coherence of agency (distributed modeling, friction reduction), N.I.K.O.System as coherence of connection (on-chain legibility, scale-coherent expansion), and source-blind convergence detection as coherence protection (identity-stripped reasoning evaluation) (Section 6).

This paper is the epistemological bedrock of a four-paper specification. The companion paper on progress (Welch, 2026a) establishes *that* mutualism is the shape toward which complex adaptive systems converge. The paper on growth (Welch & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026) specifies the mechanisms by which cooperative systems achieve superior resource allocation. The paper on governance (Welch, 2026c) provides the constitutional constraints that prevent capture, including self-capture. This paper answers the question the others presuppose: *why* mutualism works.

The answer is coherence. Systems that maintain accurate models of their constituents can predict consequences, correct errors before they cascade, and learn from non-convergence. Systems that substitute simulations for coherent models accumulate prediction errors until suppression fails and the system collapses under the weight of all the information it was not processing.

The Great Filter, in this framing, is not a technology problem. It is not a governance problem. It is not a resources problem. It is a coherence problem: the failure to extend accurate modeling — love — across the full boundary of the system upon which civilization depends. Every civilizational crisis is, at root, the arrival of consequences that the civilization's model failed to predict because the domains producing those consequences were treated as simulated rather than real.

The S(M~h)C architecture is designed to make this failure mode structurally incoherent. Not by asking participants to be more loving, but by designing systems in which simulation-distance is unproductive, coherence is the path of least resistance, and non-convergence is the primary mechanism for identifying where the model needs to expand.

Whether this design succeeds is an empirical question. The architecture is testable. The predictions are falsifiable. The code is open-source.

What is not an empirical question is the diagnosis. Extractive systems fail because they cannot model accurately what they extract from. This is information theory, not ideology. The only open question is whether a civilization can build systems that choose coherence before the accumulated prediction errors become unsurvivable.
