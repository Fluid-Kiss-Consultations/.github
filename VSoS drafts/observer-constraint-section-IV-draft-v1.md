§III closed with a specific partition: the evidence-routing pattern it described is cognitively available to any observer whose access to the target domain proceeds through causal mediation, whose construction process instantiates the predictive-processing architecture, and whose cognitive ecology affords competing explanatory resources. The question I take up here is whether the same routing pattern is also logically permitted — whether auxiliary-hypothesis deployment has structural cover independent of cognition, as a feature of the logic of confirmation itself, and not merely as a contingent feature of the architecture that happens to run on human brains. The answer philosophy of science gave well before cognitive science named the mechanism is yes. The holism of confirmation, in the form developed by Duhem (1906/1954) and generalized by Quine (1951), underwrites the routing as a property of evidence-theory relations. Not as permission-in-principle and failure-in-practice; as a logical structure that cannot be deleted without a corresponding revision of the account of how evidence confirms theory at all.

Duhem's observation about experimental testing in physics was that no hypothesis is tested alone. A scientist testing a claim against an experimental result tests it together with the assumed behavior of the instruments, the assumed absence of relevant interfering factors, the assumed correctness of the background theory that makes the measurement interpretable, and the assumed reliability of the specific auxiliary hypotheses linking the claim to an observable outcome. When the experiment disagrees with the prediction, logic alone does not tell the scientist which member of the tested bundle is at fault. The scientist may revise the core claim, or revise an auxiliary, or revise the background theory, or revise the calibration of the instruments, or revise some combination. Quine generalized the observation beyond experimental physics: any statement can be held true come what may, provided sufficient adjustments are made elsewhere in the web of belief. The holism is not license for irrational belief; it is a fact about the structure of confirmation. Evidence underconstrains the locus of revision because confirmation runs through networks, not through isolated statements.

The routing pattern §III described as cognitively available is therefore not an idiosyncratic feature of the predictive-processing architecture. It tracks a feature of how evidence bears on theory. An observer confronted with disconfirming information has, as a matter of logic, more than one place to put the revision; the holism of confirmation guarantees it. Which place the revision actually lands is a separate question, and depends on which auxiliaries the observer has cognitive access to, which revisions seem locally coherent with the rest of the observer's commitments, and which the observer's community will recognize as legitimate. The cognitive architecture selects from a structurally richer space than any single disconfirming result narrows. The Duhem-Quine move is not that disconfirming evidence never updates theory; it is that the update's location is not fixed by the evidence. This is the logical register of the routing pattern: what §III called cognitive availability has, sitting beneath it, a logical availability that the architecture does not manufacture but inherits.

Stanford (2006) gives the strongest single anchor for the section. Reviewing the history of theoretical science, Stanford documents a regularity: scientific communities confronted with a live explanatory question repeatedly fail to conceive viable theoretical alternatives that, from later vantage, turn out to be serious contenders for the truth. The failures are not distributed across eccentric fringe actors; they are systematic failures of the communities whose institutional role is precisely to conceive such alternatives. Stanford's new induction does not claim that theorizing is arbitrary, and it does not claim that any particular theory is under-supported against some specific rival. It claims something narrower and harder to dismiss: that the space of theoretical alternatives a community can generate is consistently smaller than the space of alternatives that turn out, historically, to be viable, and that there is no principled reason to think the present is an exception to the pattern. The claim reads back onto §§II-III. Stanford's argument is historical-epistemological; the framework reads the historical record through the cognitive architecture §II described. Theorizing is shaped by what the community's cognitive architecture can generate, and the architecture inherits the construction-and-precision-weighting features §II established. If the architecture is predictive, generative, and bounded, the space of theories it produces under any given epistemic condition is bounded by what its generative model can propose. Stanford's historical record is the empirical trace of that boundedness at the community level.

The framework does not require the strongest available form of the underdetermination thesis. The contemporary taxonomy distinguishes several — the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry (Stanford SEP) canonically maps the landscape — and Norton (2008) has argued persuasively that strong universal underdetermination, the claim that any theory is empirically equivalent to indefinitely many rivals, is harder to establish than often assumed; his arguments stand as the most rigorous contemporary skepticism about blanket underdetermination claims. Two responses are available, and I take the second. The first would be to engage Norton at the level of the universal claim and defend it; that is not this paper's argument. The second is to notice that the framework's argument does not need the universal claim. What it needs is a domain-conditioned version: that *when* a domain's substrate is inaccessible to direct causal contact — the translation-only-zone condition §III specified — the holism of confirmation is sufficient to establish that auxiliary-hypothesis routing is logically available. This is a weaker claim than Norton targets. Duhem-Quine holism, restricted to the translation-only zone and combined with the pattern documented in Stanford 2006 at the community level, is sufficient to underwrite the routing pattern §III describes. The framework concedes Norton's point and does not need what the point denies.

The cognitive and the logical converge. §II established the architecture that constructs rather than receives; §III showed that the architecture's precision-weighting machinery permits self-sealing as a cognitively available response under translation-only-zone conditions; §IV has shown that the same routing is logically permitted by the holism of confirmation, independent of the cognitive register, and that Stanford's new induction supplies the empirical record of the pattern at the community level. The two availabilities are not redundant. Cognitive availability without logical availability would imply that the routing survives only as a mistake the architecture makes despite the logic; logical availability without cognitive availability would imply that the routing is a possibility the architecture cannot actually execute. What the framework needs is both: a pattern the architecture can perform and a pattern the logic permits, such that the routing is neither a failure nor a curiosity but the structurally expected response to a specific epistemic condition. The question §V takes up is where, in contemporary physics, that condition obtains — and what the work looks like, at the foundations of the discipline, when it does.
