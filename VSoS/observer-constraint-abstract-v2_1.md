## Abstract

Self-sealing reasoning structures in certain domains of theoretical physics are formally identical, not merely analogous, to documented cognitive mechanisms of delusional ideation — mechanisms characterized in clinical psychiatry and operating dimensionally across the population. I argue that the identity is not coincidence but a necessary consequence, given the architecture, of epistemic underdetermination operating on a causally bounded observer.

The argument proceeds across four layers. Contemporary neuroscience establishes that perception is constructive: the brain is a hierarchical prediction machine under a free-energy-minimization imperative (Clark 2013; Friston 2010). Clinical psychiatry has operationalized self-sealing belief under the Bias Against Disconfirmatory Evidence paradigm, with dimensional extension across nonclinical populations and integration of iterative and amortised inference (Woodward et al. 2006, 2007; Harding et al. 2024). Philosophy of science establishes that evidence underdetermines theory, so any theory can be maintained against disconfirmation by auxiliary adjustment (Duhem 1906/1954; Quine 1951; Stanford 2006). And within contemporary fundamental physics, the same structure is being formalized directly along four independent strands (Wolpert, Rovelli, and Scharnhorst 2025; Rovelli 2025; Wolfram 2023; Elshatlawy, Rickles, and Arsiwalla 2025).

No prior work bridges these literatures at this register. I argue the bridge is not merely available but required: the architecture that constructs reality and that theorizes about domains beyond direct causal mediation are the same architecture. When that architecture operates on domains where observation is possible only through translated causal residue, self-sealing reasoning is not a failure mode; it is the expected output. I illustrate by applying the framework to the superdeterminism / retrocausality / standard-quantum-mechanics debate (Hossenfelder and Palmer 2020; Adlam et al. 2024), showing it is diagnostic of the debate's structure without adjudicating its physics. Four reviewer objections — pathologizing, self-application, unfalsifiability, and paradigm-stability relabeling — are taken up in turn. The framework applies symmetrically across the landscape, including to this paper's author; recognizing the architecture does not free us from it, but naming it accurately may allow the community to make better-calibrated decisions about where progress is available and where it is not.
