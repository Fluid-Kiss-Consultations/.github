§II closed on two consequences of the predictive-processing architecture: that domains whose substrate is not causally contactable can be modeled only through translation, and that the precision-weighting machinery stabilizing perception against noise becomes available as machinery for stabilizing belief against disconfirmation. Where disconfirming evidence carries direct causal contact with the substrate being modeled, that second availability mostly stays dormant; disconfirming evidence arrives, is weighted as informative, propagates as prediction error, and the model updates. Where direct causal contact is absent, the architecture lacks that grounding pressure, and the second availability shifts from exception to default disposition. The question this section takes up is what the system does under those conditions — and why clinical psychiatry, studying the pattern in cases where direct causal contact *is* available, has characterized it in terms the framework needs.

The phenomenon has a name and a paradigm. Woodward et al. (2006) formalized what they called the Bias Against Disconfirmatory Evidence, or BADE, in a task where participants rate the plausibility of candidate interpretations of an ambiguous lead-in, receive additional information that either confirms or disconfirms their initial rating, and rate again. Participants with active delusions show systematically smaller adjustments after disconfirming information than control participants, while the corresponding effect on confirming information is substantially smaller. The disconfirming evidence is not missing, not misunderstood, and not denied in any behavioral sense. It is available to the participant, and their behavior relative to it does not update in the way a Bayesian-normative account predicts. The measurement is behavioral, the phenomenon is operationalized, and the pattern has been reproduced across samples and variants of the task. BADE is not a description of introspection; it is a coordinate on a scoring rubric.

Sterzer et al. (2018), representative of a broader family of predictive-processing accounts of delusion formation, integrate this finding into the architecture §II establishes. On their account, delusions arise when precision-weighting within the predictive-coding hierarchy becomes pathologically skewed — prior beliefs receive elevated precision, incoming prediction errors receive depressed precision, and the result is a generative model that resists update even when sensory or evidential signals would, under normative weighting, drive revision. The mechanism is not a novel failure mode grafted onto the normal architecture; it is the normal architecture with its weighting skewed past a threshold. BADE, on this reading, is not a distinct cognitive process but the predictive-processing system's evidence-integration machinery operating with a distribution of precision weights that is self-protective rather than self-correcting. The pathology is quantitative. Whatever produces a clinical case produces, at smaller magnitudes, the ordinary failure of an otherwise competent observer to update a model they have already settled on.

McLean et al. (2017) consolidate the empirical picture. Their meta-analysis of BADE and related evidence-integration impairments across studies in delusion-prone and psychotic populations finds a robust correlation: the magnitude of bias against disconfirmatory evidence tracks the severity of delusional ideation across samples, methodologies, and operational variants of the task. The effect is not an artifact of any one measurement instrument. It survives the kind of scrutiny that distinguishes signal from noise in clinical cognitive research. What Woodward et al. (2006) identified in a single paradigm, and what Sterzer et al. (2018) located within the predictive-processing architecture, shows up reliably wherever the measurement is attempted.

The move the framework needs is dimensional, not categorical. Woodward et al. (2007) tested BADE in nonclinical samples scoring high on delusion-proneness — individuals with no psychiatric diagnosis — and recovered the same pattern. The bias against disconfirmatory evidence did not appear only in clinically-ill populations with the trait maximally expressed. It appeared across the distribution, scaling with delusion-proneness as a continuous cognitive disposition. This is the finding that lets the framework apply without pathologizing. The mechanism is not a clinical curiosity present only in diagnosed individuals. It is a dimensional property of human cognitive architecture, elevated in clinical presentations but operative — at lower magnitudes, in narrower domains, under specific conditions — across the general population. Every cognitive system that runs the predictive-processing architecture §II describes has the precision-weighting mechanism available to misfire. Whether it misfires, and how strongly, and in what domain, is a separate question.

Harding et al. (2024) extend the account with a hybrid predictive coding model that distinguishes iterative from amortised inference. Iterative inference is the moment-to-moment error-driven updating §II described. Amortised inference is pattern-completion from a stored model — fast, feedforward, computationally cheap, and structurally resistant to local perturbation. The amortised component explains why entrenched beliefs are harder to dislodge than their evidential history alone predicts: they do not require iterative re-derivation each time they are drawn on, and disconfirming evidence must overcome not only current predictions but the cached commitments the amortised system supplies.

Structurally, BADE routes disconfirming evidence into explanatory auxiliaries — the evidence is reinterpreted within an expanded model, discounted against higher-precision priors, or localized to a source whose relevance can be contested — rather than permitting it to update the core. That routing pattern is not confined to clinical populations. It is cognitively available to any observer whose access to the target domain proceeds through causal mediation, whose construction process instantiates the predictive-processing architecture, and whose cognitive ecology affords competing explanatory resources. The cognitive availability is established here. Whether the same routing pattern is also logically permitted — independent of cognitive architecture, as a feature of evidence-theory relations themselves — is the subject §IV takes up.
