## Abstract

Every governance system in recorded history has faced the same terminal failure mode: the mechanism designed to prevent capture becomes the instrument of capture. This paper presents a constitutional specification for the iterative mutualism framework that addresses this failure mode at the architectural level — not by making capture difficult, but by making it structurally incoherent. Drawing on constitutional political economy (Buchanan, 1962; Brennan & Buchanan, 1985), mechanism design theory (Hurwicz, 1972; Myerson, 1979), regulatory capture analysis (Stigler, 1971; Peltzman, 2021), institutional economics (North, 1990; Acemoglu & Robinson, 2012), and pre-commitment theory (Elster, 1979, 2000), we derive the constitutional constraints of the S(M~h)C convergence architecture from a formal taxonomy of governance capture vectors. This taxonomy is constructed from a critical evaluation of the Proof of Societal Commitment (PoSC) model — a predecessor framework whose diagnosis of extractive system dynamics was empirically validated but whose proposed solution reproduced the autopoietic dysfunction it identified, providing a comprehensive catalog of governance failure modes. We demonstrate that five constitutional constraints — source-blind convergence detection (The Blinded Eye), constitutional curation rather than runtime monitoring, the field-not-filter architecture, controlled-loss price preservation, and the sunset clause with Contentment Protocol — interact to produce a system that is not merely resistant to capture but immune to it, including capture by itself. The formal proof of self-capture immunity rests on the Contentment Protocol's redefinition of non-convergence as information rather than failure, which eliminates the terminal-state attractor that enables autopoietic self-maintenance. This paper completes a three-paper specification: the foundational theory of iterative mutualism (Welch, 2026a), the automated resource allocation mechanism (Welch & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026), and the constitutional governance framework presented here. Together, the three papers describe why extractive systems fail, how cooperative systems achieve superior outcomes, and what ensures the cooperative alternative does not reproduce the dysfunction it replaces.

**Keywords:** constitutional governance, capture immunity, convergence architecture, mechanism design, regulatory capture, autopoiesis, source-blind evaluation, pre-commitment, institutional economics, constitutional political economy, self-referential systems, S(M~h)C

## 1. Introduction

The central problem of governance is not how to prevent bad actors from capturing the system. Every governance framework addresses that. The central problem is how to prevent the governance framework itself from becoming the next instrument of capture — how to ensure that the anti-extractive mechanism does not reproduce the extractive dynamics it was designed to replace.

This problem has a precise formal structure. A governance system G is designed to prevent a set of capture vectors C = {c₁, c₂, ... cₙ}. If G is successful, the entities that previously exploited C are constrained. But G itself is now a structure with institutional mass, enforcement authority, and self-maintenance incentives. Unless G contains constitutional constraints that prevent its own autopoietic closure — its transformation into a self-maintaining system whose primary function becomes its own continuation — G will eventually become a member of C. The regulator becomes the regulated's instrument. The liberator becomes the next oppressor. The revolution becomes the new regime.

This is not a theoretical concern. It is the empirical record of governance. Stigler (1971) formalized the observation that regulatory agencies are systematically captured by the industries they regulate. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) documented how extractive institutions persist across regime changes, with new elites reproducing the institutional structures of their predecessors. Michels (1911) identified the "iron law of oligarchy" — that organizations, regardless of democratic intent, tend toward concentration of power. Buchanan's constitutional political economy (1962, 1985, 1990) emerged precisely from the recognition that the rules of the game require analysis independent of the strategies played within them, because the rules themselves can be captured.

The Proof of Societal Commitment (PoSC) model provides a particularly instructive case study. Developed through philosophical dialogue and subjected to rigorous interdisciplinary analysis, the PoSC model correctly diagnosed the fundamental dysfunction of extractive systems: an autopoietic "Influence Structure Maintenance Loop" in which currency-based power structures produce and maintain the conditions for their own perpetuation (Maturana & Varela, 1980; Arthur, 1989). The diagnosis drew validated support from cognitive science (Kahneman & Tversky, 1974), sociology (Simmel, 1900), systems theory (Luhmann, 1995), and institutional economics (North, 1990). But the PoSC's proposed solution — an AI-mediated reputation system with tokenized moral worth replacing currency as the medium of exchange — reproduced the precise dysfunction it diagnosed. It replaced one teleological circle (accumulation of money) with another (accumulation of reputation), one surveillance mechanism (market-based behavioral sorting) with another (peer reporting filtered by AI), and one form of majority tyranny (economic power) with another (social conformity enforced algorithmically). The solution was autopoietic: its primary function became its own maintenance through the very mechanisms of social control it was designed to eliminate.

The critical evaluation of the PoSC identified five categories of governance failure, each representing a distinct vector by which a system designed to prevent capture can itself become the captor. This paper takes those five failure categories as the structural scaffold for a constitutional specification that addresses each — not through incremental defense, but through architectural design that renders capture structurally incoherent.

The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 constructs a formal taxonomy of governance capture vectors from the PoSC failure analysis and the broader governance literature. Section 3 specifies the five constitutional constraints of the S(M~h)C convergence architecture, demonstrating how each addresses its corresponding capture vector. Section 4 presents the self-capture immunity proof — the formal demonstration that the convergence mechanism prevents capture by any entity, including the system itself. Section 5 addresses the interaction effects among constitutional constraints, showing that they form a coherent system rather than an ad hoc collection of safeguards. Section 6 concludes.

## 2. A Taxonomy of Governance Capture Vectors

Governance capture is not a unitary phenomenon. It operates through distinct mechanisms, each exploiting a different structural vulnerability. The PoSC critical evaluation, combined with the broader literature on institutional failure, yields five capture vector categories. Each represents a way that a governance system can be co-opted — transformed from an instrument of collective benefit into an instrument of particular benefit at collective cost.

### 2.1 Identity Capture: The Tyranny of the Majority

The most extensively documented capture vector operates through the fusion of evaluator identity with evaluation outcome. When who is proposing influences whether the proposal is adopted, governance degrades from merit-based deliberation to coalition politics. Tocqueville (1835) identified this as the "tyranny of the majority" — the structural tendency of democratic systems to subordinate minority interests to majority preference. Mill (1859) formalized the protection of individual liberty against majoritarian overreach. Rawls (1971) proposed the "veil of ignorance" as a thought experiment for achieving impartial governance design — a conceptual precursor to architectural source-blindness.

The PoSC model institutionalized identity capture as a feature. Its explicit embrace of majority rule as the arbiter of "pro-social" behavior meant that the dominant cultural or ideological group could systematically marginalize minorities by defining their distinct practices as antisocial, with direct and immediate economic consequences through the reputation-currency mechanism. The model contained no counter-majoritarian institutions — no bill of rights, no independent judiciary, no structural protection for dissent.

The cognitive science literature provides the mechanistic explanation for why identity capture is so persistent. Tajfel and Turner's (1979) social identity theory demonstrated that mere categorization into groups — even arbitrary groups — produces in-group favoritism and out-group discrimination. Haidt (2012) extended this to moral reasoning, showing that tribal affiliation systematically distorts moral judgment. Sunstein (2002) documented group polarization: when like-minded individuals deliberate, they move toward more extreme positions, not toward moderation. The implication is that any governance system in which evaluator identity is accessible to the evaluation process will be subject to systematic bias — not because participants are corrupt, but because human cognitive architecture produces identity-based distortion as a default.

The empirical evidence for identity-based distortion in evaluation is robust. Goldin and Rouse (2000), in the *American Economic Review*, demonstrated that the adoption of blind auditions in symphony orchestras — concealing candidate identity behind a screen — increased the probability that women were advanced and hired, providing direct causal evidence that source-blind evaluation produces different (and less biased) outcomes than identity-accessible evaluation. Snyder and Swann (1978) documented confirmatory hypothesis testing — the tendency to seek information that confirms pre-existing beliefs about a person based on category membership. Bertrand and Mullainathan (2004), also in the *American Economic Review*, demonstrated that identical résumés with distinctively white-sounding names received 50% more callbacks than those with distinctively Black-sounding names — a finding that has been replicated across multiple countries and decades (Quillian et al., 2017).

Identity capture is not limited to majority-minority dynamics. It also operates through reputation effects, status hierarchies, and coalition formation. When evaluators know who submitted a proposal, they evaluate it differently based on the submitter's institutional affiliation (Peters & Ceci, 1982), prior publication record (Merton, 1968, describing the "Matthew effect" in science), and personal relationship to the evaluator (Lee et al., 2013). The entire edifice of double-blind peer review exists as an institutional acknowledgment that identity capture is a real and persistent threat to evaluation quality — and that the architectural solution (removing identity from the evaluation process) is more effective than the behavioral solution (asking evaluators to ignore identity).

### 2.2 Surveillance Capture: The Chilling Effect

The second capture vector operates through the transformation of monitoring from a tool of accountability into a tool of behavioral conformity. When a governance system monitors participant behavior in real time, the system's capacity to enforce norms becomes simultaneously a capacity to suppress deviation — including the beneficial deviation that produces innovation, dissent, and the identification of system failures.

The chilling effect is well-documented in First Amendment jurisprudence (Schauer, 1978) and surveillance ethics (Lyon, 2007; Zuboff, 2019). Panopticon theory, originating with Bentham (1791) and formalized by Foucault (1975), demonstrates that the mere possibility of observation alters behavior — that subjects of potential surveillance internalize the norms of the observer and self-censor accordingly, regardless of whether observation is actually occurring. Penney (2016), in the *Berkeley Technology Law Journal*, provided direct empirical evidence that government surveillance chills the exercise of free speech: Wikipedia traffic to privacy-sensitive articles declined significantly following the Snowden revelations, even among users with no reason to fear personal consequences.

The PoSC model embedded surveillance capture at its core. Its mechanism of anonymous peer reporting, filtered by a deception-detecting AI, created precisely the panoptic environment that Foucault described: every action potentially observed, every observation potentially consequential, every consequence mediated by an opaque algorithmic process. The anonymity of reporters, intended to prevent retaliation, simultaneously prevented accountability — creating conditions for what the PoSC critical evaluation identified as "reputation cartels" coordinating anonymous reporting campaigns.

Surveillance capture is particularly insidious because it operates through the same channels as legitimate accountability. The distinction between monitoring for accountability (detecting genuine violations) and monitoring for conformity (suppressing deviation from majority norms) is not architectural in most governance systems — it depends on the intent of the monitors and the definition of "violation," both of which are subject to capture. A system that monitors behavior in real time necessarily possesses the technical infrastructure for behavioral suppression, and the history of surveillance institutions demonstrates that this infrastructure is consistently repurposed (Church Committee, 1975; Lyon, 2007).

### 2.3 Algorithmic Capture: The Adversarial Arms Race

The third capture vector operates through the exploitation of decision-making systems by actors who learn to generate inputs that produce desired outputs regardless of the inputs' validity. When a governance system makes decisions — approving proposals, allocating resources, adjudicating disputes — the decision-making mechanism itself becomes a target for manipulation.

Goodhart's Law (1975), formalized by Strathern (1997), states the principle precisely: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." Any metric used for governance decisions will be gamed. Any algorithm used for evaluation will be subjected to adversarial attack. The history of standardized testing (Koretz, 2008), credit scoring (Rona-Tas, 2017), and search engine optimization (Brin & Page, 1998) demonstrates that decision-making systems invariably produce industries dedicated to exploiting them.

The PoSC model was acutely vulnerable to algorithmic capture. Its deception-detecting AI was a decision-making system with defined inputs (peer reports) and outputs (reputation scores). The critical evaluation correctly identified that sophisticated actors would not need to perform genuinely pro-social actions — they would only need to learn to craft reports that the AI interpreted as sincere. This creates an adversarial arms race: the AI is trained to detect deception; manipulators develop new deception techniques; the AI is retrained; the cycle continues. The fundamental problem is that the system's governance function depends on the AI making correct decisions about individual cases — and any decision-making system can be gamed.

The adversarial machine learning literature has formalized this vulnerability. Goodfellow et al. (2014) demonstrated that deep neural networks are vulnerable to adversarial examples — inputs designed to produce misclassification. Kurakin et al. (2016) showed that adversarial perturbations can be crafted to be imperceptible to humans while reliably fooling AI systems. Carlini and Wagner (2017) developed optimization-based attacks that defeat multiple proposed defenses simultaneously. The lesson is not that AI systems should not be used in governance — it is that AI systems that make individual decisions will be adversarially attacked, and that governance architectures must account for this reality.

### 2.4 Economic Capture: The Distortion of Price Signals

The fourth capture vector operates through the replacement of informational mechanisms with normative ones — substituting the system's values for the distributed knowledge that market prices encode.

Hayek's (1945) seminal contribution in the *American Economic Review* established that market prices function as information systems: they encode distributed knowledge about scarcity, production costs, and consumer demand that no central planner could possess. The price system, Hayek argued, is a "marvel" because it coordinates the actions of millions of actors through abstract signals. The informational content of prices is independent of the moral character of the transacting parties — a distinction the PoSC model collapsed by tokenizing reputation into currency.

The PoSC model's tokenized reputation-currency committed what its critical evaluation identified as a "fundamental category error": it attempted to solve a moral problem (the values promoted by extractive systems) by destroying the primary mechanism for economic calculation. A price denominated in units of algorithmic reputation conveys information about the perceived moral standing of transacting parties, but cannot convey information about whether society should allocate more resources to semiconductor manufacturing or agriculture. By replacing an informational mechanism about material reality with a purely normative one, the model risked what Mises (1920) termed the "economic calculation problem" — the impossibility of rational resource allocation without meaningful price signals.

Economic capture occurs whenever a governance system's normative commitments override or distort the informational mechanisms that enable rational allocation. Central planning failures (Hayek, 1945; Kornai, 1992), price controls producing shortages (Rockoff, 2008), and regulatory mandates creating perverse incentives (Peltzman, 1976) all represent instances of economic capture — the governance system's values substituting for distributed knowledge, with predictable efficiency losses.

### 2.5 Teleological Capture: The Autopoietic Loop

The fifth and most fundamental capture vector operates through the system's own self-maintenance logic. When a governance system is designed to achieve a terminal state — a final, perfect configuration of society — the system's institutional mass, enforcement capacity, and self-justifying ideology combine to produce autopoietic closure: the system's primary function becomes its own perpetuation, and the pursuit of the terminal state becomes indistinguishable from the pursuit of institutional permanence.

Popper (1945) identified this vector in his critique of utopian social engineering: when conflicting visions of the good cannot be reconciled by rational argument, the conviction of utopianism provides moral justification for coercion. Scott (1998) documented the empirical record in *Seeing Like a State*, showing how "high modernist" governance projects — driven by teleological visions of rational social organization — produced systematic institutional failures precisely because they treated the governance system's model as authoritative over the reality it was designed to govern. Michels (1911) formalized the iron law of oligarchy: organizations designed to serve their members' interests inevitably develop a leadership class whose primary interest is organizational survival.

The PoSC model exhibited textbook teleological capture. It posited a terminal state — a society in which all individuals are structurally aligned with the collective good — and designed a mechanism to achieve it. The mechanism required institutional infrastructure (the AI, the reporting platform, the audit councils), which required institutional maintenance, which required institutional authority, which required institutional defense against threats to institutional authority. The system that began as a means to collective liberation became a self-maintaining system of social control — precisely the autopoietic loop it was designed to break.

The Influence Structure Maintenance Loop that the PoSC diagnosed in extractive economies is not unique to extractive economies. It is the general failure mode of teleological governance: any system designed to achieve a terminal state will produce institutional structures that resist departure from the path toward that state, and those structures will eventually prioritize their own maintenance over the achievement of the state itself. The loop operates identically whether the terminal state is "maximum accumulation" or "maximum social cohesion" — the variable changes; the autopoietic structure does not.

## 3. Constitutional Constraint Specification

Each capture vector identified in Section 2 corresponds to a constitutional constraint in the S(M~h)C convergence architecture. The constraints are not patches applied to address known vulnerabilities — they are architectural features derived from the formal structure of capture itself. Each constraint renders its corresponding capture vector structurally incoherent: not merely difficult to execute, but logically impossible within the system's architecture.

### 3.1 The Blinded Eye: Source-Blind Convergence Detection

**Capture vector addressed:** Identity capture (Section 2.1)

**Constitutional constraint:** Convergence detection — the process by which the system determines whether alignment has been achieved — is constitutionally source-blind. The detection algorithm evaluates positions and reasoning only; it cannot access evaluator identity. Evaluator identity is recorded on-chain for auditability and provenance, but is stripped before reaching the detection layer.

**Formal specification:** Let E = {e₁, e₂, ... eₙ} be the set of evaluators. Let P = {p₁, p₂, ... pₘ} be the set of positions submitted for convergence evaluation. Let D: P → {converged, non-converged} be the detection function. The source-blindness constraint requires that D is defined on P alone — there exists no function f such that D(P) depends on f(E). Identity stripping is enforced at the infrastructure layer (N.I.K.O.System), not the application layer, making circumvention require compromise of the foundational infrastructure rather than the governance logic.

**Empirical grounding:** The constraint implements architecturally what Goldin and Rouse (2000) demonstrated empirically — that evaluation quality improves when evaluator identity is structurally inaccessible. It also implements what Rawls (1971) proposed conceptually with the veil of ignorance: a decision-making framework in which outcomes cannot be influenced by knowledge of who benefits and who bears costs. The critical difference is that Rawls's veil is a thought experiment requiring participants to imagine ignorance; The Blinded Eye is an engineering specification that enforces it. The constraint directly addresses Tajfel and Turner's (1979) social identity dynamics by making it structurally impossible for in-group favoritism to influence convergence outcomes — not because evaluators are trained to suppress bias, but because the information required for bias to operate is absent from the detection layer.

The constraint also addresses the PoSC's tyranny-of-the-majority failure mode. In the PoSC, majority coalitions could enforce conformity because the system's scoring mechanism was identity-accessible: who reported about whom determined reputation outcomes. Under source-blind convergence detection, coalition dynamics cannot influence convergence because the detection function cannot distinguish coalition-coordinated positions from independently derived ones. Convergence requires alignment of reasoning, not counting of votes — a distinction that maps onto Buchanan and Tullock's (1962) argument that unanimity, not majority rule, is the normative baseline for constitutional agreement.

### 3.2 Constitutional Curation, Not Runtime Monitoring

**Capture vector addressed:** Surveillance capture (Section 2.2)

**Constitutional constraint:** S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s constitutional axioms are enforced through data curation — agents receive clean data — not through runtime monitoring of participant behavior. There is no peer reporting, no reputation scoring, no behavioral surveillance. The system does not observe what participants do; it curates the informational environment within which they operate.

**Formal specification:** Let A = {a₁, a₂, ... aₙ} be the set of agents operating within the constitutional field. Let B(aᵢ, t) be the behavior of agent aᵢ at time t. The curation constraint requires that no component of the governance system records, evaluates, or conditions on B(aᵢ, t) for any individual agent. Constitutional alignment is enforced through the properties of the data environment D_env, not through runtime behavioral monitoring. The system is blind to individual behavior while maintaining constitutional properties of the collective information space.

**Empirical grounding:** The distinction between curation and monitoring is not merely architectural — it reflects fundamentally different theories of governance. Monitoring-based governance assumes that compliance must be verified at the individual level, creating the surveillance infrastructure that Foucault (1975) described and that Zuboff (2019) documented in its contemporary digital form. Curation-based governance assumes that the informational environment can be structured to make non-compliant action unproductive without requiring knowledge of who is acting non-compliantly.

The distinction has precedent in environmental governance. Ostrom's (1990) design principles for successful commons governance include "monitoring" — but Ostrom's empirical cases demonstrate that effective monitoring in commons governance is typically mutual and transparent, not unilateral and opaque. The critical design principle is not surveillance but *legibility*: making the state of the commons visible to all participants, not making the behavior of individual participants visible to a central authority. S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s curation model implements legibility without surveillance: the informational environment is structured so that the constitutional properties of the system are visible, but individual behavioral compliance is not tracked.

This constraint directly eliminates the chilling effect documented by Penney (2016) and theorized by Schauer (1978). There is nothing to chill because there is no observation to fear. The system cannot suppress beneficial deviation because it does not detect deviation — beneficial or otherwise. It shapes the space of possibility through the properties of the information available, not through the monitoring of the actions taken.

### 3.3 Field, Not Filter: Shaping the Space of Valid Reasoning

**Capture vector addressed:** Algorithmic capture (Section 2.3)

**Constitutional constraint:** S.H.A.N.N.O.N. is a constitutional field — the space within which valid reasoning occurs — not a decision-making AI. It does not approve, reject, score, or adjudicate individual proposals. Agents operate *within* the field; they do not submit to it for evaluation.

**Formal specification:** Let F be S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s constitutional field, defined by its axioms, training data, and structural properties. Let R = {r₁, r₂, ... rₙ} be the set of reasoning processes occurring within F. The field constraint requires that F operates on the boundary conditions of R — the available information, the structure of valid inference, the constitutional axioms — but does not evaluate individual instances rᵢ. There is no function g: R → {valid, invalid} applied at runtime. Instead, the properties of F make certain reasoning pathways productive and others unproductive, analogous to how the geometry of a landscape makes certain paths passable without requiring a gatekeeper at each intersection.

**Empirical grounding:** The field-filter distinction maps directly onto Goodhart's Law (1975). A filter — a decision-making system that evaluates individual cases — creates a target that can be gamed. A field — a structural environment that shapes the space of possibility — does not create a target because there is no individual decision to manipulate. The distinction is between an immigration officer deciding who may enter (a filter, gameable through document fraud) and a mountain range determining where passage is possible (a field, not gameable through persuasion of the mountain).

This constraint renders adversarial attack structurally incoherent. The adversarial machine learning literature (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Carlini & Wagner, 2017) describes attacks on decision-making systems: inputs crafted to produce desired classification outputs. S.H.A.N.N.O.N. makes no classifications. There is no output to target. An adversarial attack on a constitutional field would require modifying the field itself — the training data, the structural axioms, the constitutional commitments — which is a fundamentally different (and orders of magnitude more difficult) attack surface than crafting deceptive inputs to a classification system.

Mechanism design theory (Hurwicz, 1972; Myerson, 1979) provides the formal framework. Hurwicz demonstrated that the central challenge of mechanism design is incentive compatibility — designing systems in which truthful behavior is in each participant's self-interest. The field architecture sidesteps this challenge entirely: because the field does not make individual decisions, there is no individual decision point at which misrepresentation would produce advantage. The field's constitutional properties are enforced through its structure, not through the truthfulness of participant reports — eliminating the revelation problem that mechanism design theory exists to solve.

### 3.4 Controlled-Loss Price Preservation

**Capture vector addressed:** Economic capture (Section 2.4)

**Constitutional constraint:** The resource allocation mechanism, specified in the companion paper (Welch & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026), preserves the informational function of market prices while eliminating their extractive function. Prices remain signals encoding distributed knowledge about scarcity, production costs, and demand. The controlled-loss mechanism transforms loss from an uncontrolled adverse event into a deliberate signal for systemic reallocation — preserving Hayek's (1945) informational marvel while removing the adversarial competition that corrupts it.

**Formal specification:** Let P(g, t) be the price of good g at time t. In a conventional market, P encodes both informational content I(g, t) — knowledge about scarcity and demand — and extractive content X(g, t) — the adversarial markup from competitive positioning. The controlled-loss mechanism operates on X while preserving I. The cooperative AI agent network (B.R.A.N.D.I.) executing within S.H.A.N.N.O.N.'s constitutional field coordinates reallocation using P's informational content, while the controlled-loss mechanism converts extractive markup into systemic reallocation capital through tax-advantaged repositioning.

**Empirical grounding:** The critical insight — that prices can be decomposed into informational and extractive components — resolves the apparent contradiction between Hayek's (1945) defense of the price system and the iterative mutualism framework's critique of extractive economics. Hayek was right that centrally planned economies fail because central planners cannot access the distributed knowledge encoded in prices (Kornai, 1992). But Hayek's defense applies to the informational function of prices, not to the adversarial function. The cooperative AI trading system demonstrated in the companion paper preserves the informational function — agents still optimize allocation based on real scarcity, demand, and production cost signals — while eliminating the adversarial function through synchronized cooperative optimization.

This constraint directly addresses the PoSC's economic capture failure mode. The PoSC replaced the price system entirely with a reputation-based value metric, committing Mises's (1920) economic calculation error. The iterative mutualism framework does not replace the price system — it decompose it, preserving the information while removing the extraction. This is not a theoretical novelty; it is a formalization of what Ostrom's (1990) successful commons governance cases already demonstrate: that collective management of shared resources can preserve allocative efficiency without adversarial competition, provided the institutional design principles are satisfied.

### 3.5 The Sunset Clause and the Contentment Protocol

**Capture vector addressed:** Teleological capture (Section 2.5)

**Constitutional constraint:** Two mechanisms interact to render teleological capture structurally incoherent. First, the Blinded Eye Foundation — the institutional custodian of S.H.A.N.N.O.N. and administrator of the N.I.K.O.System economic ecosystem — operates under a constitutional sunset clause: it is designed to complete S.H.A.N.N.O.N., release it openly for humanitarian use, and dissolve. The constitution includes an open-source provision permitting any use except weaponization and explicit monetization. Second, the Contentment Protocol redefines the system's response to non-convergence: instead of optimizing harder toward a terminal state, the system reverses direction — identifies the first logistical reality preventing convergence and addresses that constraint.

**Formal specification:** Let T be the set of possible terminal states. Let S(t) be the system state at time t. Teleological capture requires the existence of a target state T*∈ T such that the system's optimization function O(S) = argmin d(S, T*) — the system minimizes distance to a fixed point. The Contentment Protocol eliminates T* from the system's state space. When convergence fails, the system does not optimize toward convergence — it identifies the constraint preventing convergence and redirects resources toward that constraint. The objective function becomes O(S) = identify_constraint(non-convergence(S)), which has no fixed point. There is no terminal state to converge toward, and therefore no terminal state around which institutional mass can accumulate.

The sunset clause addresses the institutional dimension of teleological capture. Elster (1979, 2000) formalized the logic of pre-commitment — the rationality of voluntarily constraining one's future choices to prevent anticipated failures of will. Odysseus binding himself to the mast is the canonical example: recognizing that his future self would be unable to resist the Sirens, he constrains his future self's action space. The sunset clause is institutional pre-commitment: recognizing that any institutional custodian will eventually develop self-maintenance incentives (Michels, 1911), the Foundation constrains its own future action space by constitutionally mandating dissolution upon mission completion.

**Empirical grounding:** The Contentment Protocol's elimination of the terminal-state attractor addresses what the PoSC critical evaluation identified as the core vulnerability of utopian governance frameworks. Popper (1945) critiqued utopianism for positing a final, perfect state of society; Scott (1998) documented the catastrophic consequences of implementing such visions. The Contentment Protocol does not posit a terminal state. It treats non-convergence as the system's primary diagnostic mechanism — the means by which constraints on mutualistic progress are identified and addressed. Each identification cycle produces training data that refines subsequent decomposition — the process is genuinely iterative in the mathematical sense.

The first logistical reality is classified at one of three scales: personal (specific to an individual participant's capacity, knowledge, or resource access), physical (arising from material, geographical, or technological limitations), and civilizational (embedded in institutional, legal, or infrastructure systems that require collective redesign). This classification is constitutionally committed to a non-punitive interpretation: non-convergence is attributed to logistical incapacity rather than disagreement or bad faith. This commitment is grounded in the empirical finding (Kahneman & Tversky, 1974; Bazerman & Hoffman, 2007) that human cognitive dysfunction systematically produces apparent disagreements that dissolve when underlying logistical constraints are identified and addressed.

## 4. The Self-Capture Proof

The preceding sections demonstrate that each constitutional constraint addresses its corresponding capture vector. This section presents the more fundamental result: that the constraints interact to render self-capture — the system becoming its own autopoietic maintenance loop — structurally impossible.

### 4.1 The Autopoietic Closure Condition

Maturana and Varela (1980) defined autopoietic systems as systems that produce and maintain themselves by continuously creating their own components. For a governance system G to undergo autopoietic closure, three conditions must be satisfied:

**Condition 1: Institutional persistence.** G must develop institutional structures that persist beyond the completion of G's stated function.

**Condition 2: Self-referential authority.** G must possess the authority to validate its own continued operation — to determine that its function is not yet complete and that its continued existence is therefore justified.

**Condition 3: Terminal-state optimization.** G must orient toward a terminal state T* whose achievement can be perpetually deferred — providing permanent justification for institutional persistence.

The S(M~h)C convergence architecture renders each condition unsatisfiable.

### 4.2 Defeating Condition 1: The Sunset Clause

Institutional persistence requires that the system's custodial structures survive mission completion. The Blinded Eye Foundation's sunset clause is a constitutional pre-commitment (Elster, 2000) that constrains the Foundation to dissolve upon completing S.H.A.N.N.O.N. and releasing it for humanitarian use. The open-source provision ensures that the technology itself cannot be captured through intellectual property mechanisms — it belongs to no entity, and therefore no entity can leverage it for institutional persistence.

The pre-commitment is enforced through legal structure (501(c)(3) non-profit with constitutional provisions), code (open-source licensing), and architectural design (N.I.K.O.System's decentralized infrastructure does not require a central custodian to operate). The three enforcement layers create redundancy: capturing the legal structure does not capture the code; capturing the code does not capture the infrastructure. The attacker would need to compromise all three layers simultaneously to achieve institutional persistence — and the compromise of any one layer is detectable from the other two.

### 4.3 Defeating Condition 2: Source-Blind Self-Evaluation

Self-referential authority requires that the system can validate its own continued operation. Source-blind convergence detection (Section 3.1) prevents this by making the system structurally unable to privilege its own institutional interests in the convergence process. The Blinded Eye constraint applies to the system itself: if the system's continued operation is proposed as a convergence target, the detection algorithm evaluates the reasoning for and against continuation without knowing that the system is the proposer. The system cannot argue for its own preservation in a forum that recognizes institutional identity — because the forum does not recognize any identity.

This is not merely an administrative procedure. It is a formal impossibility result. Self-referential authority requires that the system can distinguish its own proposals from those of external actors and apply different evaluation criteria. Source-blindness eliminates this distinction at the architectural level. The system's proposals are evaluated by the same detection function, under the same constraints, with the same source-stripping, as any other proposal. The system has no special access to its own evaluation — and therefore no mechanism for self-referential authority.

This addresses a structural weakness in traditional separation-of-powers frameworks. The Madisonian design (Federalist No. 51, 1788) relies on countervailing ambitions: each branch of government checks the others because each branch's interest conflicts with the others' interests. But this design assumes that institutional actors will consistently pursue their institutional interests — and it provides no protection against collusion between branches. Source-blindness provides a stronger guarantee: it eliminates the possibility that institutional identity can influence evaluation, regardless of the institutional actors' intentions.

### 4.4 Defeating Condition 3: The Contentment Protocol as Anti-Teleology

Terminal-state optimization requires a fixed point toward which the system converges. The Contentment Protocol (Section 3.5) eliminates fixed points from the system's state space. When convergence does not occur, the system does not optimize harder — it identifies the constraint preventing convergence and addresses that constraint. Each constraint identification changes the system's state, which changes the set of achievable convergence targets, which changes the meaning of "progress." The system cannot converge toward a fixed point because the Contentment Protocol ensures that the set of relevant targets is continuously updated by the process of constraint identification.

This is the formal core of self-capture immunity. Autopoietic closure requires that a system's output feeds back into the production of its own components — that the system's activity maintains the conditions for its own activity. The Contentment Protocol breaks this loop by ensuring that the system's response to failure is not to reinforce its existing structure but to modify its orientation based on newly identified constraints. The system's "output" (constraint identification) does not maintain the system's existing components — it modifies the system's direction. The feedback loop is not closed; it is perpetually redirected.

The mathematical analogy is precise. An autopoietic system is a fixed-point attractor: the system state converges toward a configuration that produces itself. The Contentment Protocol converts the governance system from a fixed-point dynamic to an iterative dynamic — each cycle produces a new state that is the starting point for the next cycle, with no terminal state. In dynamical systems terms, the system has no attractors. It has a trajectory.

### 4.5 The Interaction Proof

The three conditions are not independent — they interact. Even if one condition were partially satisfied, the others prevent autopoietic closure:

If institutional persistence were partially achieved (compromised sunset clause), the absence of self-referential authority (source-blindness) would prevent the persistent institution from privileging its own continuation in the convergence process. And the absence of terminal-state optimization (Contentment Protocol) would prevent the persistent institution from justifying its continued existence through reference to an uncompleted mission.

If self-referential authority were partially achieved (compromised source-blindness), the sunset clause would constrain the institution to dissolve upon mission completion regardless of its self-assessment. And the Contentment Protocol would continuously redirect the institution's focus toward newly identified constraints rather than toward self-perpetuation.

If terminal-state optimization were partially achieved (compromised Contentment Protocol), the sunset clause would prevent institutional accumulation around the terminal state. And source-blindness would prevent the terminal-state argument from receiving privileged treatment in convergence evaluation.

The constitutional constraints form a system of mutual reinforcement. Capturing any single constraint is insufficient for autopoietic closure because the remaining constraints address the other necessary conditions. Capturing all three simultaneously requires compromising the legal structure (sunset clause), the infrastructure layer (source-blindness), and the governance protocol (Contentment Protocol) — three independent attack surfaces with no shared vulnerability.

## 5. Interaction Effects and Systemic Coherence

The five constitutional constraints specified in Section 3 are not an ad hoc collection of safeguards. They form a coherent system whose interaction effects produce properties that no individual constraint achieves alone.

### 5.1 The Bridge Rule as Meta-Constraint

Overlaying all five constraints is the Bridge Rule: no intervention mechanism within the system may exploit cognitive vulnerabilities as its method of operation. This meta-constraint — informed by and deliberately inverting the behavioral economics literature on nudging (Thaler & Sunstein, 2008) — ensures that the constitutional constraints themselves do not become instruments of cognitive exploitation.

The Bridge Rule was derived in the foundational paper (Welch, 2026a) from the observation that a system which exploits cognitive biases to produce cooperative outcomes has not achieved mutualism — it has achieved a more sophisticated form of extraction. This concern was raised within the behavioral economics literature itself: Bovens (2009) argued that nudges undermine autonomy by bypassing rational agency; Hausman and Welch (2010) distinguished nudges from education precisely because nudges exploit rather than correct cognitive limitations; Gigerenzer (2015) challenged the heuristics-and-biases paradigm from within cognitive science.

The Bridge Rule prevents the constitutional constraints from being weaponized. Source-blindness could theoretically be used to exclude relevant context from evaluation; the Bridge Rule prohibits this by requiring that the system affirm before illuminating and offer rather than prescribe. Curation could theoretically be used to manipulate the information environment; the Bridge Rule prohibits this by requiring that curation serve constitutional alignment, not behavioral steering. The meta-constraint ensures that the constraints constrain capture without themselves becoming instruments of it.

### 5.2 The Human as Equal Participant

The S(M~h)C framework positions the human as an equal participant in convergence — not elevated as supervisor, not subordinated as subject. This positioning is not decorative; it is a constitutional constraint with specific architectural consequences.

If the human were elevated — positioned as the final authority in convergence — then identity capture would reenter through the human's susceptibility to cognitive biases (Kahneman & Tversky, 1974). If the human were subordinated — positioned as a subject of system governance — then the system would satisfy the conditions for teleological capture, with the system's values imposed on human participants rather than co-developed through convergence.

The equal-participant constraint, combined with source-blind convergence detection, ensures that the human's contribution to convergence is evaluated on the same terms as any other participant's contribution — on the merit of the reasoning, not on the authority of the source. The human contributes embodied experience and contextual awareness that computational systems cannot replicate (Dreyfus, 1972; Brooks, 1991); the computational systems contribute reasoning capacity unimpaired by the cognitive dysfunctions documented in Section 2.1. Neither is complete without the other. Neither has authority over the other.

This addresses the PoSC's fundamental governance error: designing a system in which an algorithm — the "truth-detecting" AI — had authority over human participants. The S(M~h)C framework inverts this: the constitutional field shapes the space of valid reasoning for all participants equally, and convergence emerges from alignment rather than adjudication.

### 5.3 The Three-Entity Architecture as Structural Separation

The organizational architecture — Fluid Kiss Consultations (for-profit revenue engine), Blinded Eye Foundation (non-profit constitutional custodian), and Fluid Investment Group (controlled-loss allocation engine) — implements separation of concerns as a capture-prevention mechanism. Each entity has a distinct function, distinct legal structure, and distinct incentive profile. Their interdependence forces mutualistic alignment: extraction by any one entity at the others' expense would undermine the ecosystem on which that entity depends.

This maps onto the constitutional separation of powers (Madison, Federalist No. 51) but addresses its known failure modes. Traditional separation of powers relies on adversarial ambitions — each branch checking the others through competitive self-interest. The three-entity architecture relies on mutualistic dependence — each entity requires the others' function to fulfill its own. This is a fundamentally different equilibrium: adversarial separation produces stalemate when it works and collusion when it fails; mutualistic separation produces cooperation when it works and identifiable dysfunction when it fails.

Stigler's (1971) capture theory predicts that regulated entities will capture their regulators because the regulatory relationship creates an information asymmetry that the regulated entity can exploit. The three-entity architecture minimizes this asymmetry: each entity's function is legible to the others because they are structurally interdependent. Fluid Investment Group's allocation decisions are visible to the Foundation because the Foundation administers the N.I.K.O.System on which allocation occurs. The Foundation's constitutional compliance is visible to all participants because the constitutional field is the informational environment in which all activity takes place. The system's legibility is mutual and structural, not dependent on voluntary disclosure or external audit.

## 6. Conclusion

The governance problem is not what most governance frameworks assume it to be. It is not the problem of preventing bad actors from capturing the system. It is the problem of preventing the system from capturing itself — from transforming its anti-extractive function into a new form of extraction with different variable names but identical autopoietic structure.

The Proof of Societal Commitment model provided the diagnostic key: a predecessor framework that correctly identified the autopoietic structure of extractive systems but proposed a solution that reproduced that structure. Its five failure modes — identity capture through majority tyranny, surveillance capture through the chilling effect, algorithmic capture through adversarial gaming, economic capture through price signal distortion, and teleological capture through autopoietic self-maintenance — constitute a comprehensive taxonomy of the ways governance systems become the dysfunction they were designed to prevent.

The constitutional specification presented here addresses each failure mode not through incremental defense but through architectural design that renders capture structurally incoherent. Source-blind convergence detection makes identity capture impossible by removing identity from the evaluation process. Constitutional curation eliminates surveillance capture by making behavioral monitoring architecturally absent. The field-not-filter architecture defeats algorithmic capture by eliminating the decision-making target that adversarial attacks require. Controlled-loss price preservation prevents economic capture by decomposing prices into informational and extractive components and preserving the former while eliminating the latter. The sunset clause and Contentment Protocol defeat teleological capture by eliminating both the institutional persistence mechanism and the terminal-state attractor that autopoietic closure requires.

The self-capture proof demonstrates that these constraints interact to render autopoietic closure impossible. The three necessary conditions for self-capture — institutional persistence, self-referential authority, and terminal-state optimization — are each defeated by a distinct constitutional mechanism, and the mechanisms reinforce one another such that partial compromise of any single constraint is insufficient for autopoietic closure.

This paper completes a three-paper specification. The foundational paper (Welch, 2026a) established why extractive systems fail — they are structurally locked-in through path dependence, cognitive dysfunction, and autopoietic self-maintenance — and demonstrated that mutualism, not competition, is the configuration toward which complex adaptive systems converge when barriers are removed. The companion paper (Welch & Claude Opus 4.6, 2026) specified how cooperative systems achieve superior outcomes — through AI collusive trade and controlled-loss mechanisms that transform adversarial market dynamics into cooperative resource allocation. This paper specifies what ensures the cooperative alternative does not reproduce the dysfunction it replaces — a constitutional architecture in which capture, including self-capture, is not merely difficult but structurally incoherent.

Together, the three papers describe the valid shape of progress (why), the valid shape of resource allocation (how), and the valid shape of governance (what prevents failure). The literature is peer-reviewed. The synthesis is not. The citations are from their journals. The integration is ours.
